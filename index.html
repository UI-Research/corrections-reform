Title of the CCTF Feature

Unchecked growth in the federal prison population has led to widespread, bipartisan agreement that our corrections system is bloated, costly, and ineffective. By harnessing this momentum, policymakers can reform the federal prison system—stemming costs and reducing dangerous overcrowding, while improving public safety. The task is difficult, but a path forward has already been laid out.

The Charles Colson Task Force on Federal Corrections, established by Congress, conducted a year-long fact-finding mission to examine challenges in the federal corrections system and develop policy solutions.

We illustrate their findings here—the drivers of prison population growth, the policies that led us to this point, the task force’s recommendations, and the reasons reform is so urgent and necessary.

Federal prison population up nearly 800 percent since 1980

The United States has the highest incarceration rate in the world, with more than 2.2 million people locked up in America’s prisons and jails. And the nation’s single largest jailer is the federal government.

Since 1980, the federal prison population has risen almost 800 percent, even though the crime rate has steadily fallen over the same period. The number of people in federal prisons peaked at nearly 220,000 in 2013, before falling to roughly 197,000 people in December 2015.

This growth comes with a price. From 1980 to 2014, the Bureau of Prisons’ budget shot up 635 percent to $6.9 billion. These funds mostly go toward the housing, infrastructure, and staff costs of incarceration, leaving little for rehabilitation and reentry efforts.

The collateral cost to families and communities is much harder to quantify. Roughly X million children in the United States have a parent in prison or jail. Children with incarcerated parents have exhibited more negative outcomes, such as poor academic performance, depression, and substance abuse, and are more likely than their peers to go to prison. And whole communities can be disrupted and destabilized when residents—often men—cycle in and out of jail and return home unable to shake the stigma of a criminal record.

What’s more, a growing body of evidence suggests that mass incarceration may actually undermine public safety.

What’s driving growth?

Today’s prison population looks very different than it did decades ago. More people are being sent to federal prison and for longer periods of time, largely due to policy changes beginning in the 1980s.

The biggest driver of growth is drug offenses, specifically longer sentences for these crimes. Every year, more than 20,000 people are admitted to federal prison for drug crimes, making it the most common type of admission.

While the number of people entering federal prison for drug crimes has stayed roughly the same since X, the population continues to grow because sentences have gotten longer, meaning fewer people are being released.

The same is true for weapon and sex offenses. People convicted of immigration offenses, however, tend to serve less time, so adjusting the number of people entering prison has a greater effect on the size of the population behind bars for these crimes.

Mandatory minimums are behind longer sentences

Mandatory minimums dictate penalties for certain drug crimes and other offenses, but many argue that they can be too inflexible and severe, sending people to prison for years for small-time offenses. Minimum penalties for drug offenses, for example, are based solely on the quantity of the drug and not whether the person charged used violence or played a big role in drug trafficking organizations.

Of the X drug offenses in FY2014, 78 percent carried a mandatory minimum penalty. Of that group, the penalty was applied at sentencing for X people. Their average expected time served is 11 years, compared with 6 years for those convicted of a drug offense not subject to a mandatory minimum.

Mandatory minimums for weapons and sex offenses can also yield long prison terms.

Other policy changes also contributed to longer sentences for those in federal prison. The Sentencing Reform Act of 1984 led to stricter federal sentencing guidelines, did away with parole, and reduced the amount that could be shaved off a sentence for good conduct.

  • Prior to mandatory minimums and these changes, about a quarter of drug offenses received probation and the average prison term was roughly 3 years. Since then, about 95 percent of drug offenses have resulted in a prison term.
  • Between 1985 and 2014, time served for drug offenses went up from 27 months to 58 months. Time served for weapon offenses increased from 24 months to 60 months.
  • Between 1985 and 2014, the probability of receiving a prison sentence over probation for a drug offense increased dramatically from 50 to 90 percent.

Recent policy changes have allowed certain defendants to avoid mandatory minimums. So far, the use of mandatory minimums and the average sentence length have decreased slightly.

A roadmap for reform

In creating a path forward for reforming the federal corrections system, the Task Force was guided by the following evidence-based principles, each informed by the overarching goal of improving public safety:

  • Sentencing decisions and correctional interventions should be individualized.
  • Correctional policy should improve public safety.
  • Incarceration, with its attendant costs to both those in prison and taxpayers, should be employed judiciously.
  • Data and research should guide practice.
  • Reforms should both address prison growth and improve public safety outcomes.

Absent greater intervention, the federal prison population is unlikely to decline further

Congress, the judiciary, and the Obama administration have taken steps to improve the federal corrections system, but the work has just begun. Though these reforms will provide some relief, they’re not enough to ease the severe overcrowding.

Reform could reduce the federal prison population by 60,000 people and save $5 billion

A year of interviews, data analysis, and research laid the foundation for a path to reform. Based on this evidence and guided by the principles above, the Task Force developed the following recommendations, which are described in greater detail in their report:

  1. At sentencing, the federal system should reserve prison beds for those convicted of the most serious federal crimes.
  2. In prison, the federal Bureau of Prisons should promote a culture of safety and rehabilitation and ensure that programming is allocated in accordance with individual risk and needs.
  3. Throughout the prison term, correctional policies should incentivize participation in risk-reduction programming.
  4. Prior to and following release, the federal correctional system should ensure successful reintegration by using evidence-based practices in supervision and support.
  5. The federal criminal justice system should enhance performance and accountability through better coordination across agencies and increased transparency.
  6. Congress should reinvest savings to support the expansion of necessary programs, supervision, and treatment.

Taken as a whole, these recommendations are estimated to reduce the federal prison population by more than 60,000 people by FY2024, compared with current population forecasts. The decrease would come largely from shortening most drug-related prison sentences. With this reduction, the federal government could save over $5 billion. Those savings could be reinvested into programs and services to help those in prison return home, avoid reoffending, and rebuild their lives.
